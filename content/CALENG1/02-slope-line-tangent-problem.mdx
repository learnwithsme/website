The slope of a line passing through the points $ (x_1, y_1) $ and $ (x_2, y_2) $ is:

$$ 
m =\frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}
$$

This can also be called the **average rate of change** from $ \left(x, f(x)\right) $ to $ (x+\Delta x, f(x+\Delta x)) $. 

<div role="alert" className="alert">
  <span class="material-symbols-outlined">info</span>
  <span>$\Delta x$ is the "change" from $x_1$ to $x_2$. Some sources write it as $h$.</span>
</div>


Using this in the formula:

$$
m=\frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}=\frac{f(x+\Delta x)-f(x)}{x+\Delta x \space \space - x}
$$

Simplifying:

$$
=\frac{f(x+\Delta x)-f(x)}{\Delta x}
$$

As $\Delta x$ gets smaller and smaller (closer to 0), the line passing through the two points starts becoming a line tangent to the original function. So, to get the **slope of the tangent line**:

$$
=\lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{f(x+\Delta x)-f(x)}{\Delta x}
$$

This formula is the **instantaneous rate of change**.

## TL;DR

The slope of the tangent line of a function is 

$$
\lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{f(x+\Delta x)-f(x)}{\Delta x}
$$

---

## Examples


Get the slope of the tangent line of $ \color{green}{f(}x\color{green})=\color{orange}{x^2+1} $ at $(\color{yellow}0,\color{fuchsia}1)$.
$$
\begin{align*}m&=\lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{\color{green}{f(}x+\Delta x\color{green})-\color{fuchsia}{f(x)}}{\Delta x}\\&= \lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{\color{orange}{(x+\Delta x)^2+1}-\color{fuchsia}1}{\Delta x} \\ &=\lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{(\color{yellow}0+\Delta x)^2}{\Delta x} \\ &=\lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\Delta x \\ m&=0 \end{align*}
$$

Get the slope of the tangent line of $ \color{green}{f(}x\color{green})=\color{orange}{x^2-9} $ at $(\color{yellow}2,\color{fuchsia}{-5})$.
$$
\begin{align*}m&=\lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{\color{green}{f(}x+\Delta x\color{green})-\color{fuchsia}{f(x)}}{\Delta x}\\&= \lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{\color{orange}{(x+\Delta x)^2-9}-\color{fuchsia}{(-5)}}{\Delta x} \\&= \lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{(\color{yellow}2+\Delta x)^2-4}{\Delta x} \\ &= \lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{(\Delta x)^2+4\Delta x+4-4}{\Delta x} \\ &= \lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{\Delta x (\Delta x+4)}{\Delta x} \\ &=\lim_{\Delta x \to 0}(\Delta x+4) \\ m&=4 \end{align*}
$$
